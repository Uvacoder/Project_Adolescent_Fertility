When young women become pregnant during their adolescent [1] years, they often face life-long consequences. In countries with the highest adolescent fertility rates, girls ages 15-19 are also less likely to finish school and enter the labor force, and more likely to get married by the age of 18.

Worldwide, over the last five decades the rate of adolescent fertility has halved – from 86 live births per 1,000 women ages 15 to 19 in 1970 to 42 in 2018. The trend in adolescent fertility has been downward in all regions across the world, albeit with large differences between countries.

Today, adolescent fertility ranges from 0.3 births per 1,000 young women in the Democratic People’s Republic of Korea to 184 births per 1,000 young women in Niger.

In 2018, 22 countries, all in Sub-Saharan Africa, exceeded 100 births per 1,000 adolescent women.

Despite marked improvements, adolescent fertility remains the highest in Sub-Saharan Africa and Latin America and the Caribbean with 101 and 62 births per 1,000 young women, respectively, in 2018.

Regional aggregate data of this type may conceal substantial differences between countries, especially when tracking progress over time. Some countries like Ghana have shown significant improvements in adolescent fertility, child marriage and keeping girls in school.

The adolescent fertility rate in Ghana gradually fell from 148 in 1960 to 66 in 2018. Girls are likely to complete secondary school, though the differences between affluent families from urban areas and low-income families from the rural parts are sharp. Now only 20 percent of women ages 20-24 are first married by the age of 18, half of the rate in 1988 which was 40 percent.

These signs of change in Ghana are indicative of the potential for progress in other parts of Sub-Saharan Africa, where child marriage and adolescent fertility rates have declined only at a modest pace. West and Central Africa have the highest prevalence of child marriage.

In Niger, where adolescent fertility rate is the highest globally, girls are likely to only go to school until the age of 10, as many are married and have children early. About 18.4 percent of girls gave birth before the age of 18. The country also has the world's highest early marriage rates, with 76 percent of women ages 20-24 first married by the age of 18 and 28 percent by the age of 15.

Underlying such high rates are socio-economic factors and entrenched social norms, particularly in Niger and northern Nigeria, where religious and cultural norms often praise women who aspire to be successful wives and mothers with large families.

The costs of child marriage are considerable for young girls, their families, and their communities. Child marriage poses a significant risk to girls' fundamental human rights, including their ability to enjoy the highest attainable standard of physical and mental health, be educated, and own property. A married girl may need to stop attending school because of societal expectations, pregnancy, and housework responsibilities such as caring for children or other relatives.
